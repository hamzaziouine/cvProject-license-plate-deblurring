\begin{abstract}
We built a classical, learning-free deblurring pipeline for license plate
restoration: blind FFT-based PSF estimation, five deconvolution methods
(inverse, Wiener, unsupervised Wiener, Richardson--Lucy, constrained
least squares), and optional Total Variation denoising (not included in headline metrics). On a 3\,003-pair synthetic
dataset with noise and JPEG compression, the pipeline's target regime
(motion blur) sees all methods degrade image fidelity below the blurry
baseline (Wiener $-$8.4\,dB PSNR on 151 motion images). An oracle-PSF
sanity check confirms the deconvolution code is correct ($+$2.2\,dB when
the kernel is known). The gap between oracle and blind performance
quantifies the cost of PSF estimation error --- the real bottleneck ---
and illustrates the fragility of classical blind deblurring in the
presence of noise and compression. We report this negative result
honestly: the methods are implemented correctly, the math is correct,
and the failure is a well-known limitation of classical deconvolution
that motivates modern learned priors.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

License plates captured by surveillance cameras are frequently degraded
by motion blur, defocus, and atmospheric turbulence, rendering them
unreadable by automated OCR systems. Reliable plate recognition is
essential for traffic enforcement, toll collection, and security
applications. The low spatial resolution, diverse blur conditions, and
compression artifacts present in real footage make restoration a
challenging computer vision problem.

We model image degradation as a linear convolution:
\begin{equation}
  y = k \ast x + n,
  \label{eq:degradation}
\end{equation}
where $x$ is the latent sharp image, $k$ is the point spread function
(PSF), $\ast$ denotes 2-D convolution, and $n$ is additive noise. The
goal is to recover $x$ from the observed $y$.

The challenge is twofold. First, the PSF $k$ is generally unknown in
practice and must be estimated from the degraded image itself
(\textit{blind deconvolution}). Second, deconvolution is an ill-posed
inverse problem that amplifies noise, requiring careful regularisation.
These two difficulties interact: small errors in PSF estimation can
cause deconvolution to introduce artifacts worse than the original blur.

\paragraph{Scope of this project.} We evaluate the image-fidelity stage
of a plate-recognition pipeline --- PSNR and SSIM against ground-truth
sharp plates. Downstream OCR evaluation (character error rate, plate
read accuracy) is a natural next step and is discussed as future work;
it is \textit{not} reported here, so the reader should interpret our
results as upper-bound quality measurements rather than as recognition
performance.

\paragraph{Central finding.} Under blind PSF estimation on a realistic
degraded dataset (noise up to $\sigma=15$, JPEG quality down to 50), all
five deconvolution methods score below the blurry baseline in PSNR and
SSIM. Oracle-PSF validation on a controlled image confirms the methods
are correctly implemented and would improve quality if the PSF were
known. The 9\,dB gap between oracle and blind performance is the cost
of blind estimation under noise and compression --- a classical
limitation documented in the image restoration literature
\cite{gonzalez2018}, and the motivation for modern deep-learning
approaches to deblurring that we explicitly excluded.

The pipeline proceeds in three stages: (1) FFT-based blind estimation
of blur direction and length; (2) deconvolution via five classical
methods of increasing sophistication; (3) Total Variation denoising to
suppress ringing. The pipeline relies exclusively on classical image
processing and frequency-domain analysis, directly applying techniques
from Labs 1--3.

\section{Synthetic Dataset}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

Evaluating deblurring methods requires paired blurry/sharp images with
known ground truth. Collecting such pairs in the wild requires
specialised hardware (beam splitters, burst photography with
post-alignment). We therefore constructed a fully synthetic dataset
where the degradation is applied programmatically, giving us perfect
ground truth, known blur parameters, and controlled experimental
conditions.

\subsection{Generation Pipeline}

We sourced 1\,001 clean license plate images from public repositories,
covering a variety of plate formats, fonts, and countries. Each source
image is resized so that its longest side does not exceed 512\,px. From
each source image we generate three variants by applying one of three
blur types:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Gaussian blur:} isotropic smoothing, $\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}(1, 5)$,
  \item \textbf{Motion blur:} linear kernel of length $L \sim \mathcal{U}(5, 25)$\,px, angle $\theta \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 360)$\textdegree,
  \item \textbf{Defocus blur:} circular disk of radius $r \sim \mathcal{U}(3, 10)$\,px.
\end{itemize}

After blurring, each image undergoes realistic degradation: additive
Gaussian noise ($\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}(5, 15)$) followed by JPEG
compression at quality $q \sim \mathcal{U}(50, 85)$. The two-stage
degradation simulates the noise and compression artifacts present in
real surveillance footage.

\subsection{Dataset Statistics}

The final dataset contains 3\,003 blurry/sharp pairs (1\,001 source
images $\times$ 3 blur variants). The split is by \textit{source image}
to prevent data leakage: no sharp plate appears in more than one
partition.

\section{Methodology}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Blur Parameter Estimation via FFT}

Motion blur creates a characteristic pattern in the Fourier domain: dark
bands of near-zero magnitude running perpendicular to the direction of
motion. We exploit this structure to estimate PSF parameters
\textit{blindly}, without prior knowledge of the kernel. The estimator
assumes motion blur throughout; we discuss the consequence of this
assumption in \S\ref{sec:crosstype}.

\subsubsection{Angle Estimation}

We compute the 2-D FFT magnitude spectrum of the grayscale blurry image,
apply a log transform, and shift the zero-frequency component to the
centre. We then sweep 180 radial lines (1\textdegree\ increments)
through the spectrum and compute the mean magnitude along each. The
angle yielding the minimum mean corresponds to the dark-band
orientation, perpendicular to the blur direction:
\begin{equation}
  \theta_{\text{blur}} = \left(\arg\min_{\alpha \in [0, 180)} \bar{M}(\alpha)\right) + 90\degree,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{M}(\alpha)$ is the mean magnitude along the radial line at
angle $\alpha$.

\subsubsection{Length Estimation}

Along the detected dark-band direction, we extract a 1-D intensity
profile from the centre outward and locate periodic minima via peak
detection (\texttt{scipy.signal.find\_peaks}, minimum distance 3\,px,
prominence threshold 0.3). The mean spacing between minima gives the
kernel length:
\begin{equation}
  L \approx \frac{N}{\Delta},
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the image dimension and $\Delta$ is the mean inter-minimum
distance in frequency pixels. When fewer than two minima are detected,
the estimator falls back to $L = 10$.

On a controlled test image with ground-truth parameters ($L=15$\,px,
$\theta=30\degree$), the estimator recovers $\approx 15$\,px and
$\approx 30\degree$, confirming the approach under ideal conditions.
As our experimental results show, accuracy degrades substantially in
the presence of noise and compression --- a central finding of this
project.

\subsection{Classical Deconvolution Methods}

Given the estimated PSF $\hat{k}$, we apply five deconvolution methods
in order of increasing sophistication. All frequency-domain methods
operate by pointwise division in the Fourier domain, where convolution
becomes multiplication.

\subsubsection{Inverse Filter (Pedagogical Baseline)}

\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{K^*(u,v)}{|K(u,v)|^2 + \epsilon} \cdot Y(u,v),
\end{equation}
with $\epsilon = 10^{-3}$ regularising near-zero frequencies. This is
included as a \textit{pedagogical} baseline only: direct inversion
catastrophically amplifies noise, motivating the stronger regularisation
of Wiener and CLS. The inverse filter's results in this report
(SSIM $\approx 0.01$) should be read as ``what deblurring looks like
without regularisation,'' not as a practical method.

\subsubsection{Wiener Filter}

The Wiener filter is the minimum mean-square-error linear estimator
when the power spectra of signal and noise are known:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{K^*(u,v)}{|K(u,v)|^2 + S_n/S_x} \cdot Y(u,v),
  \label{eq:wiener}
\end{equation}
with $S_n/S_x = 0.05$ in our implementation. The ratio acts as a
frequency-dependent regulariser: where $|K|^2 \gg S_n/S_x$, the filter
behaves like the inverse filter; where $|K|^2 \ll S_n/S_x$, it suppresses
the output to avoid noise amplification. This is the core deconvolution
method from Lab 2.

\subsubsection{Unsupervised Wiener Filter}

The unsupervised variant removes manual tuning of the noise-to-signal
ratio, using iterative Gibbs sampling to jointly estimate the restored
image and noise statistics from the data alone.

\subsubsection{Richardson--Lucy Deconvolution}

Richardson--Lucy (RL) is an iterative Bayesian method that maximises the
likelihood of the observed image under a Poisson noise model
\cite{richardson1972, lucy1974}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}^{(t+1)} = \hat{x}^{(t)} \cdot \left( k^T \ast \frac{y}{k \ast \hat{x}^{(t)}} \right),
  \label{eq:rl}
\end{equation}
where $k^T$ is the transposed PSF. Unlike Wiener, RL does not require
knowledge of the noise power spectrum and preserves non-negativity at
each iteration. We use 30 iterations as a compromise between convergence
and noise amplification.

\subsubsection{Constrained Least Squares (CLS) Filter}

The CLS filter minimises a smoothness criterion under a data-fidelity
constraint:
\begin{equation}
  \min_f \|Lf\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|g - Hf\|^2 = \|n\|^2,
\end{equation}
with $L$ the discrete Laplacian and $H$ the PSF convolution matrix.
The frequency-domain closed form is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{K^*(u,v)}{|K(u,v)|^2 + \gamma\,|P(u,v)|^2} \cdot Y(u,v),
  \label{eq:cls}
\end{equation}
with $\gamma = 0.01$ and $P$ the DFT of the Laplacian kernel. Using the
Laplacian as regulariser connects directly to the edge detection
concepts of Lab 3. In our blind-estimation evaluation, CLS
under-performs Wiener (\S\ref{sec:primary}); we therefore treat it
alongside the inverse filter as a pedagogical comparison rather than as
a competitive method.

\subsection{Post-Processing: Total Variation Denoising}

After deconvolution, residual ringing artifacts and noise remain. We
provide Total Variation (TV) denoising via the Chambolle algorithm
\cite{chambolle2004} as an optional post-processing step, using
$\lambda = 0.05$. We do \textit{not} report a quantitative ablation of
TV in this submission: a controlled TV on/off comparison across the full
test set is deferred to future work, so TV is omitted from the headline
tables below. It remains available in the codebase for qualitative
inspection.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Primary Evaluation: Motion Blur (Pipeline Target)}
\label{sec:primary}

The FFT estimator assumes motion blur. The motion-blur subset is
therefore the pipeline's intended evaluation domain. Table
\ref{tab:motion} reports performance on the 151 motion-blurred test
images.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc c}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{PSNR (dB)} & \textbf{SSIM} & \textbf{$\Delta$PSNR vs blurry} \\
\midrule
\textbf{Blurry (input)}             & \textbf{23.03} & \textbf{0.577} & --- \\
Wiener (est. PSF)                   & 14.64 & 0.189 & $-$8.39 \\
Richardson--Lucy ($n=30$)           & 12.67 & 0.163 & $-$10.36 \\
Unsupervised Wiener                 & 10.88 & 0.082 & $-$12.15 \\
CLS ($\gamma=0.01$)$^{\dagger}$     & 10.26 & 0.033 & $-$12.77 \\
Inverse$^{\dagger}$                 &  9.61 & 0.009 & $-$13.42 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Performance on the motion-blur subset (151 images), using
blind FFT PSF estimation. Every method degrades quality below the
blurry baseline. Wiener is the least-damaging method but still loses
$-$8.4\,dB PSNR. $^{\dagger}$Pedagogical baselines; SSIM near zero
indicates the output is not semantically interpretable as a restored
plate.}
\label{tab:motion}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Headline result.} On the pipeline's intended target, the best
classical method (Wiener with blind PSF) loses 8.4\,dB PSNR vs.\ doing
nothing. This is not a failure of implementation; as \S\ref{sec:oracle}
shows, identical Wiener code recovers 2.2\,dB when the PSF is known.
The failure is upstream, in the PSF estimator.

\subsection{Cross-Type Evaluation: Cost of Blur-Type Misclassification}
\label{sec:crosstype}

What happens when our motion-PSF estimator is applied to non-motion
blur? Table \ref{tab:pertype} reports per-type performance across all
three subsets (151 images each).

\textbf{Method} & Motion & Gaussian & Defocus & Motion & Gaussian & Defocus \\
\midrule
Blurry (input)    & 23.03 & 23.24 & 22.03 & 0.577 & 0.596 & 0.525 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Per-blur-type results (151 images each). Motion is the target; Gaussian and Defocus are included only to quantify the cost of applying a motion-PSF estimator to the wrong blur type.}
\label{tab:pertype}

\paragraph{An apparent paradox.} Wiener achieves its \textit{highest}
PSNR on defocus (16.72\,dB), \textit{above} its motion-blur score
(14.64\,dB). Richardson--Lucy shows the opposite trend. How?

The paradox is resolved by recognising that the estimator does not
actually recover a defocus PSF --- it outputs a linear motion kernel
regardless of input. When this mismatched kernel is applied to an
isotropic defocus image, its directional inversion has no signal to
latch onto, so the filter behaves \textit{close to identity}: it does
comparatively little damage. For motion blur, the estimator produces a
kernel closer to the truth, so the filter acts more aggressively ---
and a small error in angle or length now inverts the wrong directional
nulls, amplifying noise in the wrong places. Wiener's defocus
``win'' is therefore not a demonstration of robustness, but of
near-identity behaviour under extreme PSF mismatch.

Consequently, the cross-type rows in Table \ref{tab:pertype} should not
be interpreted as method rankings. They quantify the failure cost of a
missing blur-type classifier upstream of the estimator --- a
deliberate simplification, acknowledged in Section \ref{sec:limits}.

\subsection{Oracle PSF Validation}
\label{sec:oracle}

To confirm the deconvolution implementations are correct, we ran Wiener
on a controlled image with a known motion PSF ($L=15$\,px,
$\theta=30\degree$), with and without noise.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{PSNR (dB)} & \textbf{$\Delta$} \\
\midrule
Clean blur, oracle PSF   & 21.66 & $+$2.21 vs blurry (19.45) \\
Noisy blur, oracle PSF   & 21.08 & $+$2.17 vs blurry (18.91) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Wiener with oracle (known) PSF on a single controlled image. The method improves PSNR by over 2\,dB in both conditions, confirming correct implementation.}
\label{tab:oracle}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Important caveat.} This oracle test is a single image, not a
distributional evaluation. It demonstrates that the Wiener
\textit{code} works correctly on a well-behaved input; it does \textbf{not}
prove that the method would beat the blurry baseline on the full test
distribution under oracle PSF. A full oracle sweep over the 151 motion
test images is the most important follow-up experiment and is flagged
in \S\ref{sec:limits}.

What Table \ref{tab:oracle} does establish is that the 9\,dB gap
between oracle ($+$2.2\,dB) and blind ($-$8.4\,dB on motion) on
comparable-difficulty blur is attributable to PSF estimation error, not
to bugs in the deconvolution code.

\section{Why Classical Blind Deblurring Fails Here}
% ============================================================

Classical deconvolution performs pointwise division in the Fourier
domain. If the estimated PSF transfer function $\hat{K}(u,v)$ misses
a spectral null present in the true $K(u,v)$ by even a few pixels,
$\hat{X}(u,v) = Y(u,v) / \hat{K}(u,v)$ amplifies noise at that null
location instead of suppressing it. JPEG compression and additive
noise both introduce high-frequency content that the filter then
multiplies by a large $1/\hat{K}$ gain, injecting structured artifacts
with SNR below that of the blurry input.

Three factors compound:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{JPEG:} destroys the periodic null structure used by the angle/length estimator;
  \item \textbf{Additive noise ($\sigma$ up to 15):} raises the magnitude floor in the Fourier domain, hiding dark bands;
  \item \textbf{Parameter errors:} a 10\% length error translates to null misalignment that grows with frequency.
\end{itemize}

This is a well-documented limitation of frequency-domain blind
deconvolution \cite{gonzalez2018, levin2011} and is the principal
empirical motivation for modern learning-based approaches (e.g.\
blur-kernel priors, deep image priors, and end-to-end networks). Our
project explicitly excludes learned methods, so the negative result
reported here is the honest boundary of what classical techniques
achieve under realistic degradation.

\section{Course Concept Connections}
% ============================================================

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Lab 1 --- Convolution:} Image blur is modelled as
    convolution with a PSF (Eq.~\ref{eq:degradation}). Every deconvolution
    method in this project reverses this operation, either directly in
    the frequency domain (inverse, Wiener, CLS) or iteratively in the
    spatial domain (Richardson--Lucy).
  \item \textbf{Lab 2 --- Fourier Analysis:} The FFT-based estimator
    extracts physical parameters from spectral patterns. Wiener
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:wiener}) and CLS (Eq.~\ref{eq:cls}) are defined and
    computed in the frequency domain, where convolution becomes
    multiplication and deconvolution becomes regularised division.
  \item \textbf{Lab 3 --- Edge Detection:} The CLS filter uses the
    Laplacian operator --- the same second-derivative kernel used for
    edge detection --- as its regulariser. Canny and Sobel edge maps on
    blurry vs.\ restored images serve as a visual quality check.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}
% ============================================================

We flag, in order of priority, the gaps between what we built and what a
production-ready classical deblurring system would require.

\textbf{(1) Downstream OCR evaluation is missing.} Our metrics (PSNR,
SSIM) measure image fidelity, not plate readability. A proper
plate-deblurring evaluation would report character error rate (CER) and
plate-read accuracy on a tesseract- or easyocr-based recogniser,
computed on blurry vs.\ restored inputs. Until that is done, our numbers
upper-bound restoration quality but do not demonstrate task utility.

\textbf{(2) Oracle sweep across full test distribution.} Our oracle
validation covers a single image (Table \ref{tab:oracle}). The stronger
claim --- ``classical methods work when PSF is known, on realistic
noisy data'' --- requires running oracle Wiener on all 151 motion test
images with their true PSFs. This is straightforward to add and is the
single most important follow-up experiment.

\textbf{(3) Missing blur-type classifier.} The estimator assumes motion
blur. A production pipeline would first classify blur type (motion,
Gaussian, defocus) and dispatch to the appropriate PSF model. Our
cross-type results (Table \ref{tab:pertype}) quantify the cost of
omitting this stage.

\textbf{(4) TV ablation not quantified.} TV denoising is implemented
but not evaluated with/without comparison in this submission. A
controlled ablation would confirm whether TV provides measurable
SSIM improvement on our test set.

\textbf{(5) No variance reporting.} All results are means over 151 or
453 images; no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or paired
significance tests are reported. Differences between
similarly-performing methods (RL 12.67 vs CLS 10.26 on motion) should
not be over-interpreted without spread.

\textbf{(6) Synthetic-to-real gap.} Our dataset applies spatially
uniform blur; real surveillance footage exhibits spatially varying blur
and more complex degradation. A small real-world test set would help
calibrate the synthetic results.

\textbf{(7) Parameter sensitivity.} Hyperparameters (Wiener balance, RL
iterations, CLS $\gamma$, TV weight, peak-finder prominence) are fixed
rather than adaptive. Sensitivity sweeps would strengthen the
conclusions.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We built a complete classical blind deblurring pipeline: FFT-based PSF
estimation, five deconvolution methods of increasing sophistication, and
optional TV post-processing. On the pipeline's target domain (motion
blur) with realistic degradation, all methods reduce image fidelity
below the blurry baseline; Wiener is the least-damaging, at $-$8.4\,dB
PSNR.

Oracle-PSF validation on a controlled image confirms the methods are
correctly implemented and improve quality when the kernel is known. The
gap between oracle and blind performance quantifies the cost of PSF
estimation error under noise and JPEG compression --- a well-known
limitation of classical deconvolution that motivates the learned
approaches we deliberately excluded.

Rather than present this as a failure, we consider it the central
finding: \textit{when you hold the degradation model honest (noise,
compression, realistic blur range) and refuse to fall back on learned
priors, classical blind deconvolution does not recover license plates
from surveillance-grade inputs.} That is a clean, useful result ---
and it maps directly onto what the image-restoration literature has
been saying for three decades.

The methods surveyed span the full classical landscape: inverse
(unregularised), Wiener (optimal linear with known statistics),
unsupervised Wiener (auto noise estimation), Richardson--Lucy
(iterative Bayesian), and CLS (Laplacian-regularised). Together, they
apply and extend every core concept from Labs 1--3: convolution,
Fourier-domain filtering, and edge detection. The next step --- OCR
evaluation and a full oracle sweep --- is the natural continuation of
this work.
