\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

License plates captured by surveillance cameras are frequently degraded by motion blur, defocus, and atmospheric turbulence, rendering them unreadable by automated OCR systems. Reliable plate recognition is essential for traffic enforcement, toll collection, parking management, and security applications. Yet the low spatial resolution, diverse blur conditions, and compression artifacts present in real footage make restoration a challenging computer vision problem.

We model the image degradation process as a linear convolution:
\begin{equation}
  y = k \ast x + n,
  \label{eq:degradation}
\end{equation}
where $x$ is the latent sharp plate image, $k$ is the blur kernel (point spread function, or PSF), $\ast$ denotes two-dimensional convolution, and $n$ is additive noise. The goal is to recover $x$ from the observed degraded image $y$.

The challenge is twofold: first, the PSF $k$ is generally unknown in practice and must be estimated from the degraded image itself (\textit{blind deconvolution}); second, deconvolution is an ill-posed inverse problem that amplifies noise, requiring careful regularisation. These two difficulties interact: even small errors in PSF estimation can cause deconvolution to introduce artifacts worse than the original blur.

Our approach proceeds in three stages. First, we estimate the blur direction and length directly from the Fourier magnitude spectrum of the degraded image, exploiting the characteristic dark-band pattern produced by motion blur. Second, we reconstruct the PSF and apply five classical deconvolution methods of increasing sophistication: inverse filtering, Wiener filtering, unsupervised Wiener, Richardson--Lucy (RL) deconvolution, and constrained least squares (CLS) filtering. Third, we apply Total Variation (TV) denoising to suppress ringing artifacts. The entire pipeline relies exclusively on classical image processing and frequency-domain analysis, drawing directly from the techniques studied in Labs~1--3.

The experimental results reveal a key finding: while the deconvolution methods are mathematically correct and improve quality when the PSF is accurately known, blind estimation on a realistic degraded dataset (with noise and JPEG compression) produces PSF errors that cause all methods to score below the blurry baseline. This outcome, though initially surprising, demonstrates the fundamental sensitivity of classical deconvolution to PSF accuracy---a central theme in the image restoration literature.

\section{Synthetic Dataset}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

Evaluating deblurring methods requires paired blurry/sharp images with known ground truth. Collecting such pairs in the wild requires specialised hardware rigs (e.g., beam splitters or burst photography with post-alignment). We therefore constructed a fully synthetic dataset where the degradation is applied programmatically, giving us perfect ground truth, known blur parameters, and controlled experimental conditions.

\subsection{Generation Pipeline}

We sourced over 1\,000 clean license plate images from public repositories, covering a variety of plate formats, fonts, and countries. Each source image is resized so that its longest side does not exceed 512 pixels. From each source image, we generate three variants by applying one of three blur types:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Gaussian blur:} isotropic smoothing with $\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}(1, 5)$,
  \item \textbf{Motion blur:} linear kernel of length $L \sim \mathcal{U}(5, 25)$\,px at angle $\theta \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 360)$\textdegree,
  \item \textbf{Defocus blur:} circular disk kernel of radius $r \sim \mathcal{U}(3, 10)$\,px.
\end{itemize}

After blurring, each image undergoes realistic degradation: additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma \sim \mathcal{U}(5, 15)$) followed by JPEG compression at quality $q \sim \mathcal{U}(50, 85)$. This two-stage degradation simulates the noise and compression artifacts present in real surveillance footage.

\subsection{Dataset Statistics}

The final dataset contains 3\,003 blurry/sharp pairs (1\,001 source images $\times$ 3 blur variants). The dataset is split by \textit{source image} to prevent data leakage---no sharp image appears in more than one partition:

\section{Methodology}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Blur Parameter Estimation via FFT}

Motion blur creates a characteristic pattern in the Fourier domain: dark bands of near-zero magnitude running perpendicular to the direction of motion. We exploit this structure to estimate the PSF parameters \textit{blindly}, without any prior knowledge of the kernel.

\subsubsection{Angle Estimation}

We compute the 2-D FFT magnitude spectrum of the grayscale blurry image, apply a log transform, and shift the zero-frequency component to the centre. We then sweep 180 radial lines (at 1\textdegree{} increments) through the spectrum and compute the mean magnitude along each. The angle yielding the \textit{minimum} mean corresponds to the dark-band orientation, which is perpendicular to the blur direction:

\begin{equation}
  \theta_{\text{blur}} = \left(\arg\min_{\alpha \in [0, 180)} \bar{M}(\alpha)\right) + 90\degree,
\end{equation}

where $\bar{M}(\alpha)$ is the mean magnitude along the radial line at angle $\alpha$.

\subsubsection{Length Estimation}

Along the detected dark-band direction, we extract a 1-D intensity profile from the centre outward and locate periodic minima via peak detection. The average spacing between these minima gives the kernel length via:

\begin{equation}
  L \approx \frac{N}{\Delta},
\end{equation}

where $N$ is the image dimension and $\Delta$ is the mean inter-minimum distance in frequency pixels.

On a controlled test image with ground-truth parameters (length\,=\,15\,px, angle\,=\,30\textdegree), the estimator recovers $\approx$15\,px and $\approx$30\textdegree, confirming the approach under ideal conditions. However, as our experimental results show, the estimator's accuracy degrades substantially in the presence of noise and compression artifacts, which is a central finding of this project.

\subsection{Classical Deconvolution Methods}

Given the estimated PSF $\hat{k}$, we apply five deconvolution methods in order of increasing sophistication. All frequency-domain methods operate by pointwise division in the Fourier domain, where convolution becomes multiplication.

\subsubsection{Inverse Filter (Baseline)}

The most direct approach divides the observed spectrum by the PSF transfer function:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{Y(u,v)}{K(u,v) + \epsilon},
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon = 10^{-3}$ prevents division by zero. This method is included as a pedagogical baseline: it demonstrates that naive inversion catastrophically amplifies noise at frequencies where $|K(u,v)| \to 0$, producing severe ringing artifacts and motivating the need for regularisation.

\subsubsection{Wiener Filter}

The Wiener filter is the minimum mean-square-error linear estimator when the power spectra of the signal and noise are known:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{K^*(u,v)}{|K(u,v)|^2 + S_n/S_x} \cdot Y(u,v),
  \label{eq:wiener}
\end{equation}
where $K^*$ is the complex conjugate of the PSF transfer function and $S_n/S_x$ is the noise-to-signal power ratio (set to 0.05 in our implementation). The term $S_n/S_x$ acts as a frequency-dependent regulariser: at frequencies where $|K|^2 \gg S_n/S_x$, the filter behaves like the inverse filter; where $|K|^2 \ll S_n/S_x$, the filter suppresses the output to avoid noise amplification. This is the core deconvolution method from Lab~2.

\subsubsection{Unsupervised Wiener Filter}

The unsupervised variant removes the need to manually specify the noise-to-signal ratio. It uses an iterative Gibbs sampling procedure to jointly estimate the restored image and the noise statistics from the data alone, yielding Wiener-quality results without manual tuning of the balance parameter. This makes the method more practical for automated pipelines where noise characteristics are unknown.

\subsubsection{Richardson--Lucy Deconvolution}

Richardson--Lucy (RL) deconvolution is an iterative Bayesian method that maximises the likelihood of the observed image under a Poisson noise model \cite{richardson1972,lucy1974}. The multiplicative update rule at iteration $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{x}^{(t+1)} = \hat{x}^{(t)} \cdot \left( k^T \ast \frac{y}{k \ast \hat{x}^{(t)}} \right),
  \label{eq:rl}
\end{equation}
where $k^T$ denotes the transposed (flipped) PSF. Unlike Wiener, RL does not require knowledge of the noise power spectrum and preserves non-negativity at each iteration. We use 30 iterations as a compromise between convergence and noise amplification---too many iterations cause the algorithm to fit noise.

\subsubsection{Constrained Least Squares (CLS) Filter}

The CLS filter minimises a smoothness criterion subject to a data-fidelity constraint:
\begin{equation}
  \min_f \|Lf\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|g - Hf\|^2 = \|n\|^2,
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the discrete Laplacian operator and $H$ is the convolution matrix of the PSF. The closed-form frequency-domain solution is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{X}(u,v) = \frac{K^*(u,v)}{|K(u,v)|^2 + \gamma\,|P(u,v)|^2} \cdot Y(u,v),
  \label{eq:cls}
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the DFT of the Laplacian kernel and $\gamma = 0.01$ controls the regularisation strength. The use of the Laplacian as a regulariser is particularly noteworthy: it penalises rapid intensity variations while preserving genuine edges, connecting directly to the edge detection concepts from Lab~3.

\subsection{Post-Processing: Total Variation Denoising}

After deconvolution, residual ringing artifacts and noise remain. We apply Total Variation (TV) denoising using the Chambolle algorithm \cite{chambolle2004}:
\begin{equation}
  \min_u \int |\nabla u| + \frac{1}{2\lambda}\|u - f\|^2,
\end{equation}
where the first term penalises the total variation (sum of gradient magnitudes) and the second enforces fidelity to the deconvolved image $f$. TV preserves sharp edges while smoothing flat regions, making it well-suited for suppressing ringing artifacts. We use $\lambda = 0.05$.

\subsection{Test Set Evaluation}

Table~\ref{tab:results} summarises the performance of each method on the full test set (453 images across all three blur types), using blind FFT-based PSF estimation.

\midrule
Blurry (input)                & 22.77 & 0.566 \\
Inverse filter                & 10.33 & 0.013 \\
Wiener (estimated PSF)        & 15.74 & 0.226 \\
Unsupervised Wiener           & 12.11 & 0.103 \\

All methods score below the blurry baseline. This is a central finding: the combination of blind PSF estimation errors, additive noise, and JPEG compression artifacts causes deconvolution to introduce more distortion than it removes. Among the methods, the Wiener filter performs best (15.74\,dB), followed by Richardson--Lucy (12.43\,dB), while the inverse filter performs worst (10.33\,dB), as predicted by theory.

An unexpected observation: the Wiener filter actually achieves its \textit{highest} PSNR on defocus-blurred images (16.72\,dB) rather than motion-blurred images (14.64\,dB). This counter-intuitive result is explained by the nature of PSF mismatch: motion blur produces directional nulls in the frequency spectrum, and small errors in the estimated angle or length cause deconvolution to amplify noise at incorrect null locations. Defocus blur, being isotropic, does not exhibit this directional sensitivity---the mismatched motion PSF applies a mild, non-destructive correction rather than a catastrophic directional inversion.

To verify that the deconvolution implementations are correct, we tested the Wiener filter on a controlled scenario: a single clean license plate blurred with a known motion PSF (length\,=\,15\,px, angle\,=\,30\textdegree), with no noise or compression.

This confirms that the Wiener filter \textit{does} improve quality when the PSF is accurately known, even in the presence of noise. The gap between oracle and blind performance ($+$2.2\,dB vs.\ $-$7\,dB) quantifies the cost of blind estimation and underscores the importance of PSF accuracy in classical deconvolution.

\subsection{Effect of TV Post-Processing}

Applying Total Variation denoising after deconvolution provides modest improvements by suppressing ringing artifacts. With $\lambda = 0.05$, TV denoising improves SSIM while preserving PSNR, confirming that it smooths oscillatory artifacts without destroying recovered detail. The effect is most pronounced after Richardson--Lucy and CLS, which are the methods most prone to ringing due to their iterative and Laplacian-based regularisation, respectively.

\section{Course Concept Connections}
% ============================================================

This project integrates techniques from all three laboratory sessions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Lab~1 --- Convolution:} Image blur is modelled as convolution with a PSF (Eq.~\ref{eq:degradation}). Every deconvolution method in this project reverses this operation, either directly in the frequency domain (inverse, Wiener, CLS) or iteratively in the spatial domain (Richardson--Lucy). The project is, at its core, an exercise in understanding and inverting convolution.

  \item \textbf{Lab~2 --- Fourier Analysis and Frequency Domain:} The FFT-based blur estimation directly applies frequency-domain analysis to extract physical parameters from spectral patterns. The Wiener filter (Eq.~\ref{eq:wiener}) and CLS filter (Eq.~\ref{eq:cls}) are defined and computed entirely in the frequency domain, where convolution becomes multiplication and deconvolution becomes regularised division. The frequency spectra visualised in the notebook show how blur attenuates high frequencies and how deconvolution attempts to recover them.

  \item \textbf{Lab~3 --- Edge Detection:} The CLS filter uses the Laplacian operator---the same second-derivative kernel used for edge detection---as its regulariser. By penalising the Laplacian norm, CLS encourages solutions where edges are sharp and flat regions are smooth. We also use Canny and Sobel edge maps on blurry vs.\ restored images as a visual quality measure: strong, continuous edges indicate successful restoration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Challenges and Limitations}
% ============================================================

\textbf{PSF estimation is the bottleneck.} The oracle-vs-blind comparison (Table~\ref{tab:oracle}) demonstrates a 9\,dB gap between known and estimated PSF performance. This confirms that PSF estimation accuracy---not the deconvolution algorithms---is the limiting factor. A production pipeline would require a more sophisticated estimator, potentially combining blur-type classification with type-specific parameter estimation.

\textbf{Noise and compression amplification.} Deconvolution amplifies all frequency components, including noise and JPEG blocking artifacts. The additive noise ($\sigma$ up to 15) and lossy compression (quality as low as 50) in our degradation pipeline create a challenging scenario where deconvolution consistently reduces quality. This highlights a fundamental trade-off: more aggressive deblurring recovers spatial detail but amplifies artifacts.

\textbf{Blur-type assumption.} Our FFT estimator assumes motion blur, but the dataset contains three blur types. A robust pipeline would first classify the blur type (motion, Gaussian, or defocus) before selecting the appropriate PSF model and estimator.

\textbf{Synthetic-to-real gap.} Our dataset applies spatially uniform blur, whereas real surveillance footage exhibits spatially varying blur and more complex degradation. Bridging this gap would require real blurry/sharp pairs or spatially adaptive PSF models.

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity.} The performance of each method depends on hyperparameters (Wiener balance, RL iteration count, CLS $\gamma$, TV weight) that are dataset-dependent. An adaptive parameter selection strategy would improve robustness.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We built a complete blind deblurring pipeline for license plate restoration using exclusively classical computer vision techniques. The system performs FFT-based blur estimation, applies five deconvolution methods of increasing sophistication, and finishes with TV denoising.

The main finding is the critical importance of PSF estimation accuracy. With known PSF, the Wiener filter improves PSNR by over 2\,dB. With blind estimation on a realistically degraded dataset, all methods degrade quality below the blurry baseline. This result, while initially counter-intuitive, faithfully demonstrates the fundamental sensitivity of classical deconvolution---a well-known limitation in the image restoration literature that motivates ongoing research into robust blind deconvolution and learned priors.

The comparison across methods spans the full landscape of classical deconvolution: the inverse filter fails due to noise amplification; the Wiener filter provides the best regularised estimate; the unsupervised Wiener eliminates manual tuning; Richardson--Lucy offers an iterative spatial-domain alternative; and CLS combines frequency-domain efficiency with Laplacian edge preservation. Together, these methods demonstrate how the convolution, Fourier analysis, and edge detection concepts from Labs~1--3 apply to a real-world restoration problem.
